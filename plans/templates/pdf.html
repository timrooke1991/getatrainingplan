I'm so pumped you've decided to take on the mighty marathon. I know the distance seems daunting now, but with smart training and determination, you've so got this!

  • Base Building:
    Establish a solid aerobic base with enough weekly mileage at an easy pace. This builds endurance and prepares the body for harder training. Start with around 40% of peak weekly volume.
  • Long Runs
    Do a weekly long run starting around 10 miles building up to 20+ miles. Long runs build endurance and mentally prepare for the marathon distance.
  • Speedwork
    Incorporate tempo runs, intervals, fartleks. These help improve lactate threshold, running economy and speed. Include 1-2 speed sessions per week.
  • Recovery
    Have easy run days and complete rest days. These allow the body to adapt to training and prevent injury and burnout. Reduce mileage every 4 weeks.
  • Progression
    Gradually increase total weekly mileage by around 10% each week, up to a peak of 40-50 mpw. Increase long run by 1-2 miles each week.
  • Specificity
    Include marathon pace runs. Run portions of long runs at goal marathon pace. Practice race day fueling and gear. Mimic race day conditions.
  • Taper
    Reduce volume by 25-50% over 2-3 weeks pre-race while maintaining intensity. Allows legs to feel fresh and rested for race day.
  • Periodization
    Vary training in cycles by adjusting volume, intensity and recovery. Cycle between hard and easy weeks.
  • Strength Training
    1-2 times per week for injury prevention and running efficiency. Focus on core, hips, glutes and legs.
  • Stretching
    Stretch regularly post-run to increase flexibility and prevent injury. Focus on major running muscle groups.
  • Nutrition
    Eat a balanced diet high in complex carbs, lean protein and healthy fats. Hydrate well before, during and after runs.

SESSIONS

Varied paced runs make training more comprehensive, adaptive, safe and tailored to the specific demands of marathon racing. Following a plan with purposeful variety is key to being successful over any distance.

  • Intervals
    Intervals involve repeating shorter, faster runs with recovery periods in between. Intervals help improve speed and lactate threshold. A sample interval workout may be 6x800m at 5K pace with 400m recovery jog.
  • Fartlek
    A Fartlek run alternates faster segments with recovery periods, but in a non-structured way based on how you feel. Fartleks work on speed with less monotony. A sample fartlek may push the pace for 2 minutes, jog for 1, surge again for 3, etc.
  • Long Runs
    Long runs are 1.5-3+ hours at an easy, conversational pace. They build endurance and mental stamina needed for the marathon distance. Long runs typically make up 20-30% of weekly mileage.
  • Recovery Runs
    Recovery runs are short, slow runs usually done the day after a hard workout. They flush muscles, facilitate recovery, and prevent injury while adding mileage. Keep effort very easy on these runs.
  • Tempo
    Tempo runs are steady, moderately-hard efforts run at lactate threshold pace. They build speed and efficiency at race pace. A sample tempo may be 3-5 miles at marathon pace or slightly faster.
  • Progression Runs
    Progression runs start easy and gradually build to a faster pace throughout the run. They help improve stamina and get the body accustomed to holding faster paces. A sample progression run may be 6 miles starting at an aerobic pace and finishing at marathon pace.
